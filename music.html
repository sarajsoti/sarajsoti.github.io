Hungarian Folk
Music

Instruments

Citera

The citera, or zither, is among the oldest string folk instruments, mentioned in medieval Hungarian texts from the 13th century. It is mostly played as a solo instrument, and was used at weddings and dances because its sound was excellent to support singing.

Cimbalom

The Hungarian concert cimbalom, or hammered dulcimer, was created in Budapest after the struck chordophone common to the region was built upon in 1874. It is played by striking two beaters against the steel treble and copper bass strings.

Kecskeduda

The goat bagpipe was the military instrument of the hussars up until the 16th century. Until the 1920s, it was the preferred instrument at celebrations in much of Hungary. As the pastoral lifestyle declined, it became nearly extinct by the 1950s, but was rescued as part of the Hungarian folk revival.

Hurdy-gurdy

The hurdy-gurdy is a stringed instrument that produces sound by a hand-cranked-turned, rosined wheel rubbing against the strings. The wheel functions much like a violin bow, and single notes played on the instrument sound similar to those of a violin.

Violin

The violin has been used in Hungarian music since the 17th century when minstrels were replaced by courtly musicians. Many courts or wealthier households maintained large ensembles of musicians that included players of the violin. Today, the violin is used in many folk ensembles and it typically plays the main role in folk dance music.

Utogardon

The gardon is an archaic instrument primarily played by the Hungarians living in the Szeklerland and the Gyimes region of modern day Romania. It is typically played by a woman, often the wife of the soloist violinist. The player plucks and beats the strings with a stick.

Koboz

The koboz has a short-neck without frets, and a bent-back pegbox. It is a member of the lute family. At Moldavian Csango weddings, the violin-koboz duo were the typical instruments used. It is still in use today among the Hungarian Csango people living in Moldova.

Tarogato

The tarogato is first mentioned in Hungarian writings dating back to the 15th century. It appeared in Hungary during the Ottoman-Hungarian Wars, and had no keys until the 18th century. The modern version of the instrument was created by a man named Vencel Jozsef Schunda in the 1890s.

Musical Styles by Region

Transdanubian

To the west of the Danube, which runs north to south through the middle of Hungary, there is a variety of unique music. Some instruments like the citera, the bagpipe, and the hurdy-gurdy give this music its typical sound.

  • Music from Sarkoz
  • Somogyi Songs

Tisza/Great Hungarian Plain

Along the Tisza River in Eastern Hungary, spanning from the north to the south, different sounding music emerged characterized by more upbeat rhythms and the use of the cimbalom.

  • Music from Hajdusag
  • Mezofoldi Music
  • Szegedi Songs

Transylvania

Some of the oldest Hungarian music was preserved in the region that is now a part of Romania, sheltered by the Carpathian Mountains. As villages were separated by great distances, a multitude of music emerged. The violin is the main instrument used in these songs.
